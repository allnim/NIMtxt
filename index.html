Fighting "fake news" with provenance

Provenance refers to the history of digital content assets (image, video, audio recording, document). NIM enables consumers to make qualified decisions about the provenance of any news (short or long text). By preventing scammers and spammers from impersonating others NIM offers a simple way for the ecosystem to identify the source of news as well as the creator/provider of the news. In 2020, it was estimated that disinformation in the form of fake news costs around $78 billion annually. It’s not only costly, it’s also deeply disturbing that most people starts out to mistrust any online publishers and/or journalist unless their work can be easily referenced with established provenance.


NIM will change that!

What?

"Fake news” is a term that has many interpretation, often depending on where the definition occurs. NIMs definition of “fake news”: is a fabricated story, with no verifiable facts, sources or quotes. It might be propaganda designed to fool and mislead the reader. It might be written for economic incentives, massive headlines and text coupled with advertising.

Why?

There is many reasons for “fake news”. However, more often that not it is economical and/or political reason behind it. Scam sites that are designed to look like real news outlets containing: -Misleading photographs -Headlines that looks like they are real -Content designed to deceive and destruct -Deception and/or identity theft

How?

If you haven’t verified a news article with NIMs service, DON’T SHARE IT. Do not spread fake news and misleading information. If you are unsure of the truth in what you are reading, don’t share it. If you can verify the source, don't share it! NIM is working on making this verification easier to access.
